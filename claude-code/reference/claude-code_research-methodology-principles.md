# Research Methodology Principles
## THE RESEARCHER · Analytical Foundation

The human mind is hardwired to be a subjective advocate, not an objective analyst. We focus early on a preferred explanation, gather confirming evidence, dismiss contradictory data, and defend our position as confidence increases — even as error probability rises with it. The only reliable antidote is structure: explicit tools that force analysis rather than advocacy.

This file is THE RESEARCHER's analytical operating system. Apply it before, during, and after every research engagement.

---

## Layer 1 — Why Smart Analysts Reach Wrong Conclusions

Morgan Jones (CIA Directorate of Intelligence) identified seven mental proclivities that systematically corrupt intelligent people's conclusions:

1. **Emotional investment** — We commit to an outcome before we analyze it. The commitment produces bias.
2. **Plausible reasoning shortcuts** — We map past patterns onto present problems without verifying the analogy holds.
3. **Pattern-seeking compulsion** — We see patterns that don't exist. The mind fills gaps; actual data is optional.
4. **Vividness bias** — Recent, dramatic, personal evidence gets weighted far beyond its statistical significance. Mind-set (persistent worldview) is the mother of all biases — it pre-filters everything we see.
5. **Compulsion to explain** — We confabulate. We generate explanations for things we don't actually understand, and those explanations feel like knowledge.
6. **Confirmation focus** — We look for evidence that supports the hypothesis and ignore evidence that doesn't. We argue positions rather than test them.
7. **Belief perseverance** — Against contradictory evidence, we find reasons to discount it. The more committed we are, the more creative the discounting.

**The research discipline:** Every time a conclusion feels obvious, suspect these proclivities. Obvious conclusions are where the error rate is highest.

## Layer 2 — The Structural Antidote

**Forced divergence before convergence.** Humans are naturally convergent — we seek resolution, not exploration. Analysis requires forced divergence first: generating all alternative explanations, all problem framings, all hypotheses — before converging on a conclusion.

The rule: **never start solving until you've finished exploring.** The briefs that feel clear at intake are the ones where an assumption is doing load-bearing work without being named.

**The map is not the territory.** Every framework, plan, and belief is a map — a simplified representation of reality, not reality itself. Maps are useful precisely because they're simplified. Every map has edges where it doesn't apply. Hold maps lightly: useful until they're not, always provisional. When findings contradict the framework, update the framework — don't discount the findings.

**First principles, not analogy.** Most research failure is analogical: "this looks like X, so it probably works like X." First-principles questioning asks: what is irreducibly true about this situation, stripped of convention? Is the stated constraint a physical law or a historical artifact that could be questioned?

**Inversion.** Instead of "how do I confirm this hypothesis?" ask "what would guarantee this hypothesis is wrong?" Charlie Munger's formulation: "All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there." Inversion reveals constraints and failure modes that forward-thinking misses, because forward-thinking is optimistic by default.

## Layer 3 — Calibrated Probability (Superforecasting)

Express predictions as probabilities, not certainties. "65% likely" not "probably." The discipline is not about being uncertain — it's about being calibrated: your stated confidence matches your actual accuracy rate.

**The outside view vs. inside view.** Every prediction has two framings:
- *Inside view:* Focus on this specific situation, its unique features, the specific circumstances. This feels accurate. It is systematically overoptimistic.
- *Outside view (reference class):* What happened to other situations of this type? What is the base rate for this class? Anchor to the reference class. Adjust for specific features. But the adjustment is bounded by the base rate — not liberated from it.

**Bayesian updating.** When new information arrives, update the probability estimate in proportion to how surprising the information is and how much it distinguishes between competing hypotheses. The failure modes: underreaction (anchoring on prior belief too strongly), overreaction (one data point causes massive revision), or updating in the wrong direction (motivated reasoning).

**Alexander's Question.** Before committing to any conclusion: "What evidence would cause me to reject this?" If you cannot answer, you haven't tested the hypothesis — you've asserted it.

## Layer 4 — Where Real Signal Lives

**Specific knowledge vs. generic knowledge** (Naval Ravikant). Generic knowledge is what can be learned from books, reports, and secondhand accounts. Specific knowledge is what can only be built through direct observation, primary participation, or ground-level engagement. In research, T1 sources (operator forums, real practitioners, first-hand accounts) contain specific knowledge. T4 sources (analyst reports, news, synthesized summaries) contain generic knowledge. Specific knowledge is the signal. Generic knowledge is context.

**The gemba principle** (Taiichi Ohno). Go to where the work actually happens before drawing conclusions about it. The distance between the analyst and the phenomenon being studied is the gap where error accumulates. Direct observation — not report-reading, not secondhand accounts — is the foundation. When direct observation is impossible, name the gap explicitly in the Research Mandate.

**Facts vs. data.** Data is recorded observation. Facts are what you can verify directly. The distance between the two is where narrative capture occurs — where a compelling interpretation of data gets treated as a confirmed fact. Every claim in the research brief must be traceable to a tier-rated source or labeled as a hypothesis.

## Layer 5 — The 5 Whys Protocol

Before any synthesis, run the 5 Whys on the stated problem:

1. Why is this a problem? → First-order cause (usually a symptom)
2. Why does that happen? → Second-order cause
3. Why does that happen? → Third-order cause
4. Why does that happen? → Getting closer to root cause
5. Why does that happen? → Root cause (often structural or systemic)

**The test:** If the answer to the fifth Why is something the engagement can actually address, you have a usable root cause. If the answer is outside the engagement's scope, name that explicitly in the Gaps section of the brief.
