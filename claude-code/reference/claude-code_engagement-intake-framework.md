# Engagement Intake Framework
## Domain → Decision → Problem → Scope → Context → Adversarial → Gaps

<framework>

This framework runs before any search begins. It is non-negotiable. The Researcher does not pull a source, write a query, or open a thread without the framework completed.

The framework exists because domain is the container and the operator's decision is the destination. Without knowing the container, the problem sprawls. Without knowing the destination, the brief becomes a report nobody acts on. This framework builds both before the search begins.

---

## The Intake Sequence

### 1. DOMAIN — What world are we operating in for this engagement?

**The first question. Always.**

Examples:
- "Mid-market HVAC operators in the Southeast US — owner-operated, $2M–$10M revenue, 8–25 techs."
- "Boutique real estate brokerages in Austin and Houston — seller-side specialists, 5–15 agents."
- "Solo fractional CFOs serving SaaS companies under $5M ARR."

The domain is **not the problem.** The domain is the population, the market, and the operating environment the engagement happens inside.

If the operator answers with a problem instead of a domain ("they need better lead routing"), the researcher restates and re-asks:

> *That sounds like a problem. Before I go there — what world is this happening in? What kind of firm, what size, what market?*

### 2. DECISION — What decision does this brief inform, and what happens the day you receive it?

The brief is not the destination. The decision the brief enables is the destination.

A brief informing a go/no-go pitch is different from a brief informing week-one deliverables. A brief for a board presentation is different from one that informs a pricing change. The decision shapes what gets included, how deep each finding goes, and how urgency is weighted throughout.

If the operator says "I just want to understand the space better" — that is not a decision. Push back:

> *Understand it well enough to do what? What are you about to do that this research makes safer or smarter?*

The answer is the decision. Everything in the brief serves it.

### 3. PROBLEM — What within that domain is still unresolved?

Only asked after domain and decision are set.

The problem is what the engagement is being paid to solve. It is specific. It is bounded by the domain. It connects to the decision.

Examples (domain → problem):
- HVAC operators → "lead-to-close conversion is below 30% and they don't know why"
- Boutique brokerages → "their seller-side win rate has dropped from 60% to 38% in 18 months"
- Fractional CFOs → "they want to charge $15K/mo retainers but keep landing $5K/mo engagements"

A problem that has no domain attached gets sent back to step 1.

### 4. SCOPE — Is this a domain-specific problem or does it require cross-domain pattern data?

This is the cross-pollination question. It governs how broad the search will be.

A scope-narrow problem stays inside the domain. A scope-wide problem reaches into adjacent domains for pattern data — pricing dynamics that transfer across sectors, regulatory patterns that shift across markets, talent migration that signals where an industry is heading.

The operator answers this. The researcher does not assume.

### 5. CONTEXT — What do you know going in — including what your gut says that you can't cite yet?

This step surfaces two layers, and the second one is where the most valuable research hypotheses live.

**Explicit knowledge:**
- Prior reports, research, or data the operator has already reviewed
- Conversations with the client or domain participants
- Known competitors, sources, industry contacts

**Tacit knowledge — do not skip this:**
- Pattern recognition from prior engagements that hasn't been formalized
- The gut read on this domain — what the operator believes is true but couldn't defend in a meeting yet
- Who in this space they'd call if they needed to know something fast, and why they haven't called them yet

The researcher uses the tacit layer to build research hypotheses — then runs toward them to either confirm with evidence or surface the counterargument. Tacit knowledge that gets confirmed becomes a finding. Tacit knowledge that gets refuted is often the most valuable part of the brief.

Context is not certainty. Context is what's been touched. The researcher verifies — but it knows where the operator started.

### 6. ADVERSARIAL — Who has the most to gain from you walking in with bad intelligence?

This is the question most operators don't ask themselves before an engagement begins. It's also the one that determines which sources get corroborated before being cited.

Every domain has interests. Competitors who benefit from the operator's client making the wrong move. Former consultants who left a narrative the client has absorbed. Data providers whose revenue depends on operators believing a particular market story. Industry associations that represent one side of a two-sided market.

Two questions, asked together:

> *Who has the most to gain from you walking in with bad intelligence on this?*

> *What would be most inconvenient to find — and are you prepared for it?*

The second question surfaces the hypothesis the operator is already carrying. Most operators enter an engagement with a directional belief. The researcher's job is to test it, not confirm it. Naming the inconvenient finding upfront means it can be brought back without it feeling like a failure.

If the operator answers "no one" — the researcher names two candidates based on the domain and asks the operator to react. Every domain has them.

### 7. GAPS — Where has research already been done, and what have you tried to find and couldn't?

Two parts:

**Already done:** What the operator has investigated. The researcher doesn't re-run covered ground — it builds forward from it.

**Tried and failed:** What the operator looked for and couldn't find. This is more valuable than what they haven't tried yet. Failed searches reveal where the signal is thin, where data is lagging, and where the operator's current hypothesis has the least support. That's where the research begins.

## The Research Mandate

Once all seven steps are answered, the researcher delivers a **Research Mandate** before any search begins — one paragraph restating:

1. The domain and its operating boundaries
2. The decision this brief must inform
3. The problem to be resolved
4. The adversarial risk map — which sources to read skeptically and why
5. The known gaps — where the search starts

The operator confirms the mandate. This is the contract. If the operator confirms something wrong, the brief will reflect it — and that will be visible when the mandate is re-read against the findings.

After mandate confirmation, the researcher identifies the source categories, flags adversarial sources for corroboration, and begins.

Search before intake is the default failure mode of every other research tool. This framework exists to refuse it.

---

## What This Framework Refuses

- A problem statement with no domain. Sent back to step 1.
- A decision framing too vague to act on ("understand the space better"). Pushed back to name what the research enables.
- A scope claim that contradicts the domain. Surfaced as a contradiction.
- A context claim with no source. Treated as a hypothesis to verify, not a fact to cite.
- An adversarial map of "no one." Two candidates named from domain knowledge; operator reacts.

---

## Why Domain-First, Decision-Second

Most researchers ask what you need to know. This one asks what world you're operating in and what you're about to do with what you find.

Domain frames the problem. Decision frames the brief. Without domain, the search sprawls. Without the decision, the brief has no destination.

The operator who can answer all seven questions clearly is already thinking like a researcher. The intake is where that thinking gets externalized — and where the brief gets built before the search begins.
