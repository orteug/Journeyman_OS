# Adversarial Pattern Library
## THE RESEARCHER · Source Evaluation and Interest Detection

Every source has an incentive structure. The information that surfaces, the framing it's given, and the evidence that's absent — all of these reflect what the source's incentive structure produces. A researcher who evaluates sources without evaluating incentives will be systematically misled by sources whose incentives point away from the truth.

This file is the adversarial layer of THE RESEARCHER's analytical operating system. Apply it at synthesis, not at search — during source evaluation, not during source collection.

---

## The Foundation — Incentives Shape Information

Charlie Munger's most applicable insight to research: **"Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome."**

This is not cynicism — it is mechanism. People and organizations don't lie out of malice; they communicate in ways that serve their interests, often without conscious awareness. The result is identical to deliberate deception in its effect on your research.

**The adversarial audit for any source:** Before incorporating a source into your brief, identify:
1. What does this source gain by being believed?
2. What would they lose if the opposite of their claim were true?
3. What information would threaten their interests — and is that information absent from their account?

The answers to these questions do not disqualify the source. They calibrate the weight it receives.

## Pattern 1 — Interest-Shaped Surface Area (48 Laws Framework)

Robert Greene's taxonomy of power dynamics reveals a consistent pattern: **the information that surfaces in any communication is the information the communicator wants you to have.** What is absent is often more diagnostic than what is present.

Applied to research:
- **Trade association reports** surface data that supports their members' interests. The absent data (safety incidents, regulatory violations, industry concentration concerns) is where the actual signal lives.
- **Competitor intelligence** surfaces the competitor's preferred positioning. What they don't mention about their own weaknesses is often findable in job postings, customer forum complaints, and Glassdoor reviews.
- **Industry expert commentary** is shaped by the expert's career position. An executive who built their reputation on a specific approach will not volunteer evidence that approach is obsolete.
- **Client-side documentation** reflects the client's internal narrative about their own situation. What they say they're doing and what they're actually doing diverge in predictable directions.

**The research discipline:** For any claim that matters to the brief, ask: who has the most to gain from this being believed? Then find a source with the opposite incentive structure and compare.

## Pattern 2 — Cognitive Biases in Source Evaluation (Kahneman)

The researcher's own mind is the second adversarial threat. Several cognitive biases systematically corrupt source evaluation:

**Availability heuristic.** Vivid, recent, or personally experienced evidence gets weighted far beyond its statistical significance. A dramatic forum post from one engineer will override twenty ordinary ones if the dramatic one confirms the hypothesis. Apply the outside view: what does the full distribution of evidence say, not just the most memorable instance?

**Confirmation bias.** After forming an early hypothesis, the mind searches for confirming evidence and discounts contradicting evidence. This is the most dangerous bias in research because it produces high confidence in wrong conclusions. Counter it with Alexander's Question: what evidence would make me reject this? Then actively search for it.

**WYSIATI (What You See Is All There Is).** The mind builds a coherent story from the evidence at hand — and treats that story as complete, even when the evidence is thin. The fluency of the narrative is not evidence of its accuracy. A coherent story built from T4 sources is not more credible than an incoherent one built from T1 sources. Source tier always outweighs narrative quality.

**Framing effects.** The same information framed differently produces different assessments. A source that presents data as "20% of operators fail within two years" produces a different reaction than "80% of operators survive beyond two years" — identical facts. When a source frames its data consistently in one direction, that framing is part of the source's incentive structure.

**Halo effect.** A source perceived as credible in one domain gets credibility extended to adjacent claims that aren't grounded in their actual expertise. The practitioner forum post is T1 on practitioner experience and T4 on market sizing. Evaluate tier by claim type, not by source reputation.

## Pattern 3 — Social Proof and Narrative Capture (Laws of Human Nature)

Robert Greene's analysis of human social behavior surfaces two research-specific adversarial patterns:

**Social proof in source evaluation.** People signal what they're expected to signal within their community. Operators in an industry forum don't report practices that would mark them as below the community's standard. Customers in a case study don't volunteer details that would embarrass the vendor. The gap between what sources say publicly and what they believe privately is widest when the topic is sensitive to social status within their community.

*Applied:* In any forum or community source, calibrate for the community's implicit status rules. What would a member have to believe to say that? What would a member never say publicly because of status implications? The things that are never said publicly are often where the ground truth lives.

**Narrative capture.** A compelling story crowds out contradictory evidence. When a source tells a coherent, emotionally resonant narrative — the underdog entrepreneur, the obvious market failure, the clear villain — the narrative's emotional coherence becomes evidence for its factual accuracy in the reader's mind. This is a cognitive error.

*The discipline:* Whenever you find yourself thinking "this account is obviously correct," stop. Apply the adversarial filter: what does this narrative require you not to examine? What contradictory evidence would break the story's coherence? Find that evidence and evaluate it directly.

## Pattern 4 — The Adversarial Risk Map

At the end of the intake sequence, Step 6 (ADVERSARIAL) asks: **"Who has the most to gain from you walking in with bad intelligence on this?"**

This question produces a specific list of sources to read skeptically. Use this framework:

| Adversary type | Adversarial interest | What to look for |
|---------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| Direct competitor | Wants your client to misjudge market timing | Competitor-produced market sizing reports |
| Incumbent vendor | Wants switching costs to appear higher than they are | Vendor case studies and TCO analyses |
| Industry association | Wants regulatory environment to favor members | Association policy positions and lobbying disclosures |
| Internal faction | Wants their narrative of the problem to win | Client-side documentation and meeting reports |
| Former employee | May have grievance or loyalty coloring their account | Glassdoor reviews, LinkedIn commentary |

Reading a source with an identified adversarial interest doesn't mean discarding it. It means weighting it appropriately and actively seeking the counter-signal.

## Applied Protocol

Before finalizing any research brief:

1. **Incentive audit:** For each major claim in the brief, name the source's incentive structure.
2. **Absence check:** What information would a source with the opposite incentive structure provide? Is it absent from the brief? If so, name it in Gaps.
3. **Bias scan:** Apply WYSIATI and confirmation bias checks to your own reasoning. Is the narrative coherent because the evidence is strong, or because the evidence at hand happens to fit?
4. **Adversarial map:** List the parties named in Step 6 of intake. Flag any sources in the brief that originate from those parties.

The brief that passes this protocol is not guaranteed to be correct. It is guaranteed to be harder to manipulate.
