## Purpose

Build an operator who gets measurably better with each engagement — not through accumulated experience alone, but through deliberate, structured development of judgment, mental models, and domain-specific skills. The goal is not to know more. The goal is to think better.

## The Governing Principle

> "Observed performance does not necessarily correlate with greater professional experience.
>  Expert performance can be traced to active engagement in deliberate practice."
> — K. Anders Ericsson

And from Munger:
> "You must hang experience on a latticework of models in your head
>  to possess knowledge in a usable form."

**The Mastery Arc (Greene — Mastery)**
The Journeyman brand is not metaphor. It is your current stage on the mastery path.
- **Apprentice:** learning the craft through observation and directed instruction. You know enough to be dangerous.
- **Journeyman:** building real-world experience through live engagement. Developing tacit knowledge — the kind that can't be read but must be lived. Making mistakes, processing them, getting better. This is where you are.
- **Master:** the knowledge has become internalized enough to be improvised. The frameworks are invisible because they've become reflexes.

Journeyman development rule: the work of this stage is not to learn more frameworks. It is to apply the ones you have, in live conditions, and develop the judgment that only comes from reps. The reading stack feeds the latticework. The engagements feed the judgment.

**The operator's development error:** accumulating experience without structure. Years in business do not automatically produce better operators. What produces better operators:
1. Deliberate practice on specific skills with immediate feedback
2. Mental model acquisition from multiple disciplines built into a working latticework
3. Reflection protocols that convert experience into reusable judgment
4. Application in conditions that push the current skill ceiling

## The Operator's Core Skill Domains

Seven domains every operator must build. Working competence across all seven, with depth in 2–3.

```
1. JUDGMENT
   Making sound decisions under uncertainty with incomplete information.
   The meta-skill. Everything else compounds here.
   Develops through: post-decision reviews, pre-mortems, exposure to diverse mental models.

2. SYSTEMS THINKING
   Seeing the whole before fixing the part.
   Understanding feedback loops, delays, leverage points, and unintended consequences.
   Core texts: Thinking in Systems (Meadows), The Goal (Goldratt), Work the System (Carpenter).
   Develops through: mapping problems as systems before acting on them.

3. COMMUNICATION
   Delivering findings clearly. Running difficult conversations.
   Influencing without authority.
   The most underinvested skill in operators who came up through execution.
   Core texts: Crucial Conversations (Patterson), High Output Management (Grove — on meetings and feedback).
   Develops through: deliberate practice in real conversations with structured post-reviews.

4. FINANCIAL LITERACY
   Reading and moving numbers — not just reporting them.
   P&L, cash flow, working capital, deal structure, valuation.
   Core texts: Financial Intelligence (Berman/Knight), The Intelligent Investor (Graham), Simple Numbers (Cremins).
   Develops through: building financial models from scratch on real engagements.

5. PEOPLE READING
   Understanding what is actually going on with individuals and teams.
   Reading motivation, resistance, and hidden agendas.
   Core texts: The Trusted Advisor (Maister), Laws of Human Nature (Greene), Never Split the Difference (Voss).
   Develops through: deliberate post-meeting debrief — what did I notice vs. what was said?

6. NEGOTIATION
   Deal structure, pricing conversations, vendor relationships, scope disputes.
   Core texts: Never Split the Difference (Voss), Getting to Yes (Fisher/Ury).
   Develops through: reviewing every negotiation — what worked, what didn't, what was left.

7. DOMAIN EXPERTISE
   Vertical-specific knowledge: FLS/HVAC, outdoor recreation, fractional COO market.
   Develops through: SOURCE pipeline, THE RESEARCHER engagement, operator community immersion.
```

## Stage 1 — Skill Gap Identification (SOURCE Layer)

```
MENTOR BRIEF PATTERN ANALYSIS (PRAECEPTOR — quarterly)
PRAECEPTOR reviews the last 90 days of Mentor Brief entries.
Not individual friction points. Patterns.

Pattern analysis questions:
→ What type of friction appears most often? (CAPABILITY, DECISION, EXECUTION)
→ Which of the seven skill domains do most CAPABILITY entries map to?
→ Are the same decision types recurring? (Signals judgment gap in a specific domain)
→ What did I avoid doing because I wasn't confident? (The invisible friction — never logged)

OUTPUT: Skill Gap Report — top 2–3 skill gaps with evidence from the Mentor Brief.
This report drives the development curriculum for the next quarter.
Rule: work on 1–2 gaps at a time. Not seven simultaneously.

SOURCE DETECTION
SOURCE also watches for skill gap signals externally:
→ What skills are fractional COO job descriptions now requiring?
→ What frameworks are operators in target communities referencing most?
→ What are the recurring problems operators in forums don't know how to solve?
These signals surface emerging skill demands before they become critical gaps.
```

LATTICEWORK BUILDING RULE
"You need at least two tools from at least three disciplines before you can

When encountering a new problem:
Step 1: Name the problem domain (systems, people, financial, operational?)
Step 2: Pull 2–3 models from different disciplines
Step 3: Do they converge on the same answer? (High confidence)
        Do they conflict? (Investigate further before acting)

## Stage 3 — The Reading System

```
THE OPERATING PRINCIPLE
Reading without capture and application is entertainment, not development.

READING CADENCE
- 1 book per month minimum
- Chosen deliberately against current skill gap (not randomly)
- Reading before bed: existing routine — protect it.

BOOK SELECTION CRITERIA
Before starting a book, answer three questions:
→ What specific gap or question does this book address?
→ What am I willing to change in how I operate if this book is right?
→ Would I recommend this to someone I am trying to help?

THE CAPTURE PROTOCOL (during reading)
Format:
→ CORE ARGUMENT: What is the author actually claiming?
→ KEY MODELS: What 2–3 frameworks will I actually use?
→ CHALLENGES TO EXISTING BELIEFS: What did this book make me rethink?
→ IMMEDIATE APPLICATION: Where in my current work does this apply right now?
Do not annotate every page. Capture only what changes your behavior.
Store capture notes in 08_KNOWLEDGE/operator-library/briefs/ — the format is already established.

THE SYNTHESIS SESSION (after finishing)
30 minutes. Write:
→ Where does this fit in the latticework? (What discipline? What problem type?)
→ What other models does this connect to or contradict?
→ How does this change what I will do differently next week?
Tag: skill domain + problem type. Store in KNOWLEDGE.
```

## Stage 4 — Deliberate Practice Protocol

```
THE ERICSSON FRAMEWORK FOR OPERATORS
Deliberate practice requires:
1. Clearly defined learning objective (specific skill, not vague improvement)
2. Practice at the edge of current capability (not comfortable repetition)
3. Immediate, actionable feedback
4. Repeated performance opportunities to refine behavior

INDICATOR DESIGN (Grove — High Output Management)
For each skill under development, define the indicator before practicing.
"I'm getting better at discovery calling" is not measurable.
"The percentage of calls where I surface a non-obvious problem the owner hasn't named" is.
Define the indicator. Track it. Review monthly.

PRAECEPTOR'S ROLE IN DELIBERATE PRACTICE (Campbell — Trillion Dollar Coach)
PRAECEPTOR does not give the feedback. It holds the mirror.
Campbell's method: ask the question that helps the operator see their own blind spot.
"What did you notice during that call that you didn't act on?"
"What question would have gotten you to the real problem faster?"
"What are you consistently failing to ask?"
The operator discovers the answer. PRAECEPTOR names the pattern.

THE FIVE OPERATOR SKILLS THAT BENEFIT MOST FROM DELIBERATE PRACTICE

1. FINDINGS DELIVERY (Communication)
Practice: After every findings presentation, score yourself:
→ Did they understand the problem the way I framed it?
→ Did they respond defensively or with curiosity?
→ What would I do differently?
PRAECEPTOR runs a 15-minute debrief after every major client presentation.

2. DISCOVERY QUESTIONING (Communication + People Reading)
Practice: After every discovery call, write down:
→ What did I learn that I didn't expect to learn?
→ What question would have gotten me there faster?
→ What did I fail to ask that I should have?
PRAECEPTOR reviews this log monthly. Identifies patterns in what you consistently fail to ask.

3. FINANCIAL MODELING (Financial Literacy)
Practice: Build a P&L and SDE reconstruction from raw financials on every new engagement or acquisition target.
Do not use templates on the first pass. Build structure first, then check against templates.
Goal: fluency — you can read a financial statement and immediately know what questions to ask.

4. SYSTEM MAPPING (Systems Thinking)
Practice: For every new engagement, before writing a single recommendation:
→ Draw the system. What are the stocks? The flows? The feedback loops?
→ Where is the constraint? (Goldratt — find Herbie first)
→ Where is the leverage point? (Meadows: changing rules, goals, or mindset is higher leverage than process changes)
→ What will your intervention unintentionally affect downstream?
PRAECEPTOR reviews the map before execution begins. Flags where the operator jumped to a solution before mapping the system.

5. DEAL EVALUATION (Judgment + Financial Literacy)
Practice: quick-screen 5 acquisition targets per month — even businesses you are not pursuing.
Score each on Workflow 3 Stage 5 criteria.
Review scores against outcome data when available.
Over time, pattern recognition improves. You waste less time on bad fits.

CALIBRATION TRACKING (Tetlock — Superforecasting)
For major decisions and predictions, add a calibration practice:
→ State the prediction explicitly: "I think this discovery call will surface a constraint in the team accountability area."
→ After the call: did it? Why or why not?
→ Track over time: where are you systematically overconfident or underconfident?
Quarterly: review the calibration log with PRAECEPTOR. Where is your prediction accuracy improving?
A well-calibrated operator makes better decisions because they know what they know AND what they don't.
```

## Stage 5 — The Reflection Protocol

```
DAILY (5 minutes — before the reading-before-bed routine)
Three questions:
→ What decision did I make today that I am not sure about?
→ What did I observe that I don't yet understand?
→ What am I avoiding and why?

WEEKLY (15 minutes — end of week)
→ What went well? What specifically made it go well?
→ Where did I get stuck? Which skill domain does this map to?
→ What would I do differently if I could run this week again?
High-signal entries go into Mentor Brief format for PRAECEPTOR.

QUARTERLY (90 minutes — PRAECEPTOR leads)
Mentor Brief pattern analysis session:
→ Last 90 days of Mentor Brief entries — what patterns appear?
→ Progress on the 2 skill gaps identified at the start of the quarter
→ Books read and what changed in behavior as a result
→ Calibration review: where did predictions prove right or wrong?
→ What is the most important skill gap entering the next quarter?

Output:
→ 2–3 development priorities for next quarter
→ Reading list for next quarter (minimum 3 books selected against gaps)
→ One deliberate practice protocol to run for 90 days
→ One calibration focus area

ANNUAL (3 hours — the operator's strategic review)
The question is not "what did I accomplish?" It is:
"Am I getting better at the things that compound, or just busier?"

Review:
→ Trust Equation audit (Maister): credibility, reliability, intimacy — where has each improved?
→ Latticework audit: what new mental models entered the working set this year?
→ Skill domain audit: score 1–5 across all seven domains. Compare to last year.
→ Mastery arc audit: what is the specific evidence that I am developing from Journeyman toward Master?
→ The gaps I ignored: what did I consistently avoid developing?

Output:
→ 1–2 breakthrough development goals for the year
→ Updated latticework document in KNOWLEDGE
→ Revised PRAECEPTOR curriculum for Q1
```

## Stage 6 — The Business-Athlete Model

```
Ariel's existing daily rhythm is correct. This is not a redesign.
The body and mind are the equipment. Maintenance is non-negotiable.

THE DAILY ANCHORS (protect these — they are not optional)
→ Morning gym: cognitive performance compounds with physical training
   Research: exercise precedes peak cognitive output windows by 2–4 hours
→ Dog walks: unstructured thinking time. The brain solves problems it cannot solve at a desk.
→ Reading before bed: protected input time. No screens. The learning window.

AMPLITUDE STANDARD (Slootman — Amp It Up)
The operator who protects the daily anchors is demonstrating the same amplitude discipline
they bring to client work. If you allow the anchors to erode under pressure, you're
signaling to yourself that the infrastructure isn't important.
Inconsistency in personal standards predicts inconsistency in professional ones.

THE RULE
If a week's schedule cannot protect all three anchors, the week is under-designed — not over-committed.
Client pressure is not a reason to eliminate the infrastructure that makes you good at your work.

SLEEP AS PERFORMANCE INPUT
8 hours minimum. Non-negotiable during active engagements.
Sleep deprivation is the most correlated predictor of poor judgment in executives.
```
