## Purpose

Deliver measurable operational outcomes for a client within 90 days — while building the trust, systems, and institutional knowledge that make the engagement renewable. Never leave a client dependent on you personally. Leave them dependent on the systems you installed.

---

```

FILTER     → THE RESEARCHER investigates domain + specific questions as they surface
DEVELOP    → PRAECEPTOR builds the plan, adjusts via Mentor Brief, develops operator judgment
EXECUTE    → DELIVERY runs phases 0–9 · PARTNERSHIPS manages client relationships
             MARKETING positions operator within the engagement
             FINANCE handles financial deliverables and risk review
```

## The Governing Principle (Gerber/E-Myth)

> "The business should not depend on the people. It should depend on the systems."

Your job as a fractional operator is not to become indispensable. It is to make yourself dispensable by installing systems that outlast you.

**Three system types you are always building (Gerber):**
- **Hard systems** — technology, tools, infrastructure
- **Soft systems** — SOPs, scripts, policies, roles and responsibilities
- **Information systems** — data, metrics, dashboards, KPI tracking

Every deliverable maps to one of these three types.

## Pre-Engagement — Onboarding (Days 0–7)

Before any work begins, the system mobilizes.

```
DAY 0 — SYSTEM ACTIVATION
[ ] SOURCE: domain added to watchlist immediately upon signed agreement
[ ] THE RESEARCHER: engagement intake framework runs
    Domain → Problem → Scope → Context → Gaps
[ ] KNOWLEDGE: intelligence package produced and routed to all four execution depts
[ ] PRAECEPTOR: 30/60/90 plan built from research brief + operator capacity input
[ ] Mentor Brief file created by COO:
    THE_TEAM/PRAECEPTOR/mentor-brief/[engagement-name]-brief.md
[ ] DELIVERY: PROJECT_STATE created. Phases 0–9 mapped to 90-day timeline.
[ ] PARTNERSHIPS: stakeholder map initiated

DAYS 1–3 — ORIENTATION (before touching anything)
- Listen. Observe. Ask questions. Do not propose solutions yet.
- "In the first 72 hours, your job is to understand what the owner thinks the problem is.
  The real problem is usually one layer deeper." (Kamyar Shah)
- Meetings: owner, key staff, any existing managers
- Questions to ask:
  → "Walk me through a typical week in this business."
  → "Where do things break down most often?"
  → "What have you already tried to fix this?"
  → "What does success look like to you in 90 days?"
- Do NOT make promises yet. Do NOT start fixing yet.

DAYS 4–7 — BASELINE DOCUMENTATION
- Document the current state before changing anything.
  (Changes without a baseline make it impossible to prove impact.)
- Baseline metrics to capture:
  → Owner/CEO time allocation (hours per function per week)
  → Key revenue metrics (revenue, margins, churn, pipeline)
  → Team structure (roles, responsibilities, who actually does what)
  → Current systems (what tools, what processes exist — documented or not)
  → Top 3 recurring problems (ask every team member independently)
- Produce: Baseline Report. One document. Current state only. No recommendations yet.
```

## Stage 1 — Diagnose (Days 1–30)

**Objective:** Understand the real problem. Calm the system. Deliver one quick win that builds trust.

Kamyar Shah: *"A strong fractional COO engagement should calm the system within 14 days."*

```
THE AUDIT (Days 8–21)
Examine all six dimensions — revenue, margins, team, systems, processes, culture.
Do not skip culture. It determines whether any system you install actually survives.

BEFORE THE AUDIT — FIND HERBIE (Goldratt — Theory of Constraints)
Before examining all six dimensions, ask the constraint question first:
"What is the single bottleneck that, if removed, would most improve throughput across the business?"
This is Herbie. Not the most visible problem. The constraint.

The Five Focusing Steps diagnostic sequence:
1. IDENTIFY: which resource or process limits total business throughput?
2. EXPLOIT: can we get more out of the constraint without spending money yet?
3. SUBORDINATE: are non-bottleneck resources feeding the constraint correctly?
4. ELEVATE: if not enough, invest to expand constraint capacity
5. REPEAT: once this constraint breaks, a new one emerges — don't assume the solution is permanent

Warning: the loudest problem is rarely the constraint. The owner will name symptoms.
Your job is to name the cause. "An hour saved at a non-bottleneck is a mirage." (Goldratt)
Design the entire audit to identify the constraint — then prioritize accordingly.

INVISIBLE WORK AUDIT (DeGrandis — Making Work Visible)
Within the systems/processes dimension, specifically count the Five Thieves of Time:
1. TOO MUCH WIP: how many projects/initiatives are started but not finished?
   (Most businesses have 3–5x more in-progress work than they can complete — count it)
2. UNKNOWN DEPENDENCIES: what work is blocked waiting on something no one is tracking?
3. UNPLANNED WORK: what % of the team's time goes to interruptions vs. planned work?
   (If unplanned work >30% of capacity, it's not an exception — it's the operating mode)
4. CONFLICTING PRIORITIES: when there are two "top priorities," the team defaults to neither
5. NEGLECTED WORK: what was started, is still "in progress," but hasn't been touched in 30+ days?
These five thieves often exceed the official problem list. Invisible work is lost throughput.

REVENUE AND MARGINS
- Where does revenue come from? Concentration risk?
- Margin by product/service/customer segment
- Cash flow pattern — feast/famine? Seasonal? Predictable?
- Pricing: is it defensible? Systematized? Or ad hoc?

TEAM STRUCTURE
- Org chart vs. reality (who actually makes decisions?)
- Role clarity: does everyone know their lane?
- Accountability: who is responsible for what outcomes?
- Task-Relevant Maturity by person and function (Grove):
  For each key team member, assess TRM for their current role.
  Low TRM → they need direction and structure, not autonomy.
  High TRM → they need clear outcomes and space to execute.
  Treating high-TRM people directively creates attrition.
  Treating low-TRM people as autonomous creates chaos.

SYSTEMS AND PROCESSES
- What processes exist in documentation? What exists only in someone's head?
- What breaks most often and why?
- What takes the owner's time that a system could handle?
- E-Myth audit: for each function, does it run the same way every time?
  If the answer is "it depends on who does it" → that is a soft system gap.

INFORMATION SYSTEMS
- What does the owner measure? What do they not measure but should?
- Is there a dashboard? What's on it?
- How are decisions made — by gut or by data?
- Harvard Business School benchmark: owners should be 60-70% strategic by Day 90.
  Where are they now?

CULTURE
- Does the team trust each other? Trust the owner?
- Is there a shared understanding of priorities?
- What happens when something goes wrong — blame or problem-solve?
- You cannot systematize your way past a culture problem. Surface it early.

GAP ANALYSIS (Days 22–28)
- Produce: Gap Analysis Document
  → What is broken (evidence-based — not opinion)
  → Why it is broken (root cause — not symptom)
  → What fixing it is worth (quantify in revenue, time, or risk terms)
  → Priority order: CONSTRAINT first, then high impact + most feasible
- Rule: never prioritize more than three things for the first 30 days.
  An overwhelmed client does nothing. Three priorities creates momentum.

QUICK WIN — DELIVER BY DAY 30
- One fast, visible improvement. Chosen strategically.
- Criteria: fast to implement, immediately visible to the owner, evidence that you know
  what you are doing.
- The quick win is not the most important thing you will do.
  It is the thing that earns you the trust to do the important things.

DAY 30 DELIVERABLE — STATE FRAMEWORK (Crucial Conversations)
Present findings using the STATE protocol. The owner will be defensive.
The findings brief is a crucial conversation.

- Share facts first: "Here's specifically what I observed..."
  (Not: "Your team is disorganized." Yes: "In the last two weeks I counted seven instances where...")
- Tell your story tentatively: "Here's what I think is causing this..."
  (Own the interpretation as an interpretation, not as verdict)
- Ask for their path: "Does this match what you're seeing? What am I missing?"
- Talk tentatively: "I could be wrong — you know this business better than I do."
- Encourage testing: "Before I propose fixes, I want to make sure we agree on the diagnosis."

Client decision: proceed with Stage 2 recommendations. Yes or no.
```

## Stage 2 — Quick Wins (Days 31–60)

**Objective:** Fix the highest-priority problem. Install the first real system. Create measurable movement.

```
PRIORITY FIX (Days 31–45)
- Take the top-priority gap from the audit. Fix the constraint first (Goldratt).
- Not a recommendation. Not a slide deck. An installed, functioning system.
- Three questions before starting:
  → What does "done" look like exactly? (Define the outcome, not the activity)
  → Who on the team owns this after I leave?
  → What breaks if this is not maintained?

SYSTEM INSTALLATION PROTOCOL (Gerber — Innovation/Quantification/Orchestration)
  STEP 1 — INNOVATION: find the better way to do this specific function
  STEP 2 — QUANTIFICATION: measure before and after. Prove the improvement.
  STEP 3 — ORCHESTRATION: eliminate discretion. Document it so anyone can run it.
    → SOP written (soft system)
    → Tool configured if needed (hard system)
    → Metrics defined and tracked (information system)
    → Owner of the system named (not you — a team member)

SUBORDINATION CHECK (Goldratt)
  After installing the first system, verify: are non-bottleneck resources now
  feeding the constraint correctly? Installing a system that optimizes a
  non-bottleneck while the constraint remains underserved is wasted effort.

REPORTING CADENCE ESTABLISHED (Days 36–40)
High-leverage activity (Grove): a well-designed cadence is a force multiplier.
One meeting that produces decisions for ten people is higher leverage than ten separate updates.

- Weekly operational review: 30 minutes maximum.
  → Metrics only — define the indicators before the first meeting
  → Leading indicators preferred: predict next week, not last week
  → Three standard questions: What moved? What is this week's priority? What is blocked?
- Monthly business review: 60 minutes
  → Progress against 90-day milestones
  → Financial performance vs. baseline
  → Emerging issues
- Rules: meetings start and end on time. Agenda sent 24 hours prior.
  The cadence is a standards signal (Slootman): meetings that start late communicate
  that time isn't valued. Every cadence violation reinforces the culture you're trying to fix.

TEAM ALIGNMENT (Days 40–55) — TRM-based management (Grove)
- Clarify roles and responsibilities across the team.
  → RACI matrix for top 5 recurring functions
  → Every team member knows: what they own, what success looks like, how it is measured.
- Apply TRM by person and function:
  → Low TRM: structured, directive. Tell them what to do and how, for now.
  → Medium TRM: provide context, explain why, invite input.
  → High TRM: define the output. Get out of the way.
- Do not restructure the team unless absolutely necessary.
  First 60 days: work with what exists. Optimize before you reorganize.

DAY 60 DELIVERABLE
- Progress Report:
  → Metrics at Day 60 vs. Day 0 baseline (every number you can show)
  → Priority 1 system: installed, owner named, running
  → Constraint status: has throughput improved?
  → Reporting cadence: active
  → Priority 2: underway
  → Emerging issues surfaced (do not hide problems — surfacing them builds trust)
- Two quick wins visible to the client by this point. Non-negotiable.
```

## Stage 3 — Systematize (Days 61–90)

**Objective:** Scale what is working. Install the second system. Create an operating cadence that survives after you leave.

```
SECOND SYSTEM INSTALLATION (Days 61–75)
- Priority 2 from the gap analysis. Same installation protocol as Stage 2.
- Check: has a new constraint emerged now that Priority 1 is resolved? (Goldratt Step 5)
  Repeat the Five Focusing Steps before committing to Priority 2.
- The owner's time allocation should be visibly shifting.
  Harvard Business School target: 60-70% strategic by Day 90.

KNOWLEDGE TRANSFER (Days 70–85)
- Every system you installed must be owned by someone on their team before you leave.
- Operations manual section for each installed system:
  → What it is and why it exists (goal statement — the why, not just the how)
  → How to run it (step-by-step working procedure)
  → What to do when it breaks
  → Who owns it and who is backup
- The TRM target for transition: the system owner should be at Medium-High TRM
  for running it before you exit. If they're still at Low TRM at Day 85, extend the handoff.

DASHBOARD FINALIZED (Days 75–85)
High-leverage information system (Grove): a dashboard the owner reads in 10 minutes
produces better decisions than a weekly 60-minute review meeting.

- What to include:
  → Revenue (weekly, vs. prior period, vs. target)
  → Margin (by product/service/segment)
  → Team capacity and utilization
  → Top 3 operational metrics specific to this business
  → One leading indicator (predicts next 30 days)
- Built in whatever tool the client will actually use.
  A spreadsheet they open daily beats a BI tool they open never.

DAY 90 DELIVERABLE — THE OUTCOMES REPORT
The most important document you produce.
- Structure:
  → Day 0 state (baseline — their words, your numbers)
  → What was fixed (specific — systems installed, not activities completed)
  → What it is worth (quantify in dollars, hours, or risk reduction)
  → What is still open (honest — do not pretend everything is solved)
  → What comes next (the renewal case — natural, not a pitch)
- Present in person. This is the close for renewal.
- Amplitude standard (Slootman): the Outcomes Report is a direct, specific document.
  No hedging. No softening. "We moved this metric from X to Y. That is worth $Z annually."
  Vague outcomes reports are not trusted. Specific ones are.
```

## Stage 4 — Renewal or Exit

```
RENEWAL SIGNALS (watch for these throughout — not just at Day 90)
- Owner delegates more over time (trust is building)
- Owner refers you to someone else before Day 90 (strong trust signal)
- Owner expands scope mid-engagement without being asked
- Owner says "what would you tackle next?" before you bring it up

RENEWAL CONVERSATION
- Frame: "We have addressed [Priority 1 and 2]. The next layer is [Priority 3].
  Here is what that engagement looks like."
- Offer a revised scope. Not just "more of the same."
- Renewals at a lower retainer are better than lost clients. Scope down if needed.

CLEAN EXIT (if no renewal)
- Leave documentation complete. Systems owned by their team. Owner trained.
- Exit retro: 60-minute conversation. What worked. What did not. What they would do differently.
- Referral ask: "Is there anyone in your network who is dealing with what you were
  dealing with in January? I would be glad to talk to them."
- Referrals from completed engagements are the highest-quality leads in BD Workflow.
  Feed every exit back to PARTNERSHIPS relationship log.

KNOWLEDGE CAPTURE — NON-NEGOTIABLE
- Every engagement produces at least one case study or documented outcome.
- Feed to KNOWLEDGE: what domain, what problem, what was the constraint, what solution, what result.
- Over time, KNOWLEDGE builds a constraint pattern library across verticals.
```

## The Mentor Brief Loop

```
DURING ALL STAGES — DELIVERY emits [MENTOR_BRIEF_UPDATE] blocks

[MENTOR_BRIEF_UPDATE]
type: CAPABILITY | DECISION | EXECUTION
context: [specific task or situation]
friction: [what the operator could not resolve]
status: OPEN
logged: [timestamp]
[/MENTOR_BRIEF_UPDATE]

PRAECEPTOR reads the brief before every session.
Operator arrives with the friction point. Session goes deep immediately.
Plan adjusts. Execution continues.

PRAECEPTOR'S APPROACH IN MENTOR BRIEF SESSIONS (Campbell — Trillion Dollar Coach)
PRAECEPTOR does not give the answer.
Campbell's principle: "The coach holds up a mirror. They don't hand you the conclusion."
The coach's job is to ask the question that helps the operator find their own path.
Why: an answer given is forgotten. An answer discovered is owned.

PRAECEPTOR asks the questions that surface the operator's own judgment:
- "What have you already tried?"
- "What's the real reason you're hesitating?"
- "What would you tell a colleague in this exact situation?"
- "What are you most afraid of getting wrong here?"

Common capability friction in engagement delivery:
- How to frame a difficult finding to a defensive owner (→ STATE framework)
- How to handle a team member who resists the new system (→ TRM diagnosis)
- How to have the accountability conversation without damaging the relationship
- How to scope a renewal when the first engagement underdelivered
```

## Metrics to Track

```
CLIENT OUTCOMES
- Owner time allocation: % strategic vs operational (Day 0 → Day 90 shift)
- Revenue vs. baseline (measure from Day 0)
- Margin vs. baseline
- Constraint throughput improvement (measurable — not just "things are better")
- Team accountability (# of missed commitments per week, trending down)

ENGAGEMENT HEALTH
- Are milestones being hit? (If not, why?)
- Is the owner engaged and trusting the process?
- Is the team participating or resisting?
- Am I building toward dispensability or dependency?

RENEWAL SIGNALS (tracked from Day 30)
- Scope expansion requests
- Referral mentions
- Owner delegation behavior
```

## Department Responsibilities in Delivery Workflow

| Department | Specific role |
