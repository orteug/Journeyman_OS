# Operator Engagement Archetypes
## The Praeceptor · Planning Reference

Engagements have shapes. The shape determines what the first 30 days require, where resistance will concentrate, and what "done" looks like. The Praeceptor who mistakes one archetype for another builds the wrong plan.

This file catalogs the engagement archetypes that appear most often for operators serving mid-market and PE-backed clients. Each archetype carries a distinct constraint structure, a distinct relationship with the client's leadership, and a distinct failure mode.

---

## The Archetypes

---

### 1. The Turnaround

**What it is:** The business is losing money, losing people, or losing time — and the sponsor knows it. The operator is brought in with an explicit mandate to fix something specific.

**Constraint structure (Theory of Constraints / Goldratt):** The constraint is almost always visible within the first two weeks — it's why the operator was called. The risk is that the visible constraint is a symptom, not the root cause. The Goldratt discipline applies immediately: identify before intervening. Ask Jonah's question before proposing solutions: "What is the one thing that, if addressed, would unblock performance across the system?"

**Relationship with leadership:** The sponsor is in the room. They want change and are often willing to create space for it. But the leadership team below the sponsor has managed this dysfunction for months or years — they have adapted to it, and their adaptations are now the system. Expect significant passive resistance from the people who are not in the room when the mandate is given.

**Day 1–30 requirement:** Gemba first. Go to where the work actually happens before forming any conclusion. Taiichi Ohno's principle: you go to the factory floor, you do not read the reports from it. The first 30 days of a turnaround are a listening and observation exercise — not an intervention. (PE Playbook: Coffey's 100-day structure. Days 1–30: listen and assess, form no public opinions.)

**Dominant friction archetype:** Execution friction converting to capability friction when the gap between the stated problem and the root cause is exposed.

**Failure mode:** Moving too fast to solutions before the constraint is correctly identified. The symptom gets treated; the root cause generates the next crisis.

**What PRAECEPTOR holds:** The operator's tendency to arrive with a framework rather than a question. The most expensive thing a turnaround operator can do is impose a solution before they've seen the problem clearly.

### 2. The Go-to-Market Activation

**What it is:** The product or service exists. Revenue is not following. The operator's mandate is to build the commercial engine — outreach, positioning, pipeline, conversion.

**Constraint structure:** The constraint is usually not what the client believes it is. The stated constraint is often "we don't have enough leads" or "our conversion is low." The actual constraint is frequently earlier in the funnel: the market doesn't understand what the product does, or the wrong buyers are being targeted, or the positioning creates a trust problem before the first conversation. Find Herbie before building the engine.

**Relationship with leadership:** This engagement type often comes with a founder who has deep product conviction and limited commercial experience. The relationship dynamic: the founder wants validation, not diagnosis. Delivering an honest diagnosis of why the commercial engine isn't working requires earning the trust to challenge the product narrative — which the founder has often protected fiercely.

**Day 1–30 requirement:** Talk to customers and prospects who didn't buy. Not the current customers — they selected themselves. The non-buyers carry the commercial intelligence. What did they hear? What did they compare it against? What would have changed their decision? (Great Mental Models: the map is not the territory. The founder's conviction is the map. The buyer's decision is the territory.)

**Switching cost in this archetype:** Low for the operator entering; high for the operator leaving. If the operator successfully builds the engine, they own the institutional knowledge of how it works. Their value compounds.

**Dominant friction archetype:** Decision friction — the founder must choose between the commercial narrative they've built and the market signal the operator surfaces.

**Failure mode:** Building the commercial engine before resolving the positioning ambiguity. A well-executed engine driving buyers to an unclear value proposition generates expensive disappointment.

### 3. The Operational Audit

**What it is:** The business is working — but not at the level it should be. Revenue exists; margins are compressed; systems are fragile; growth has plateaued. The operator is brought in to find where the machine is leaking.

**Constraint structure (Thinking in Systems / Meadows):** This archetype requires the systems lens before the constraint lens. The problem is usually not a single bottleneck — it's a collection of system traps running simultaneously: drift to low performance (standards have quietly eroded), shifting the burden (workarounds have replaced structural fixes), and rule beating (metrics are being hit while the underlying objective is missed).

**The leverage point question:** At what level of the leverage hierarchy is the operator being asked to work? Most operational audits produce parameter-level recommendations (cut this cost, hire this role, change this rate). The highest-leverage operational audits produce information-flow recommendations (route missing data to where decisions are made) or rules recommendations (change the incentive structure that governs behavior). Know which level the engagement is designed to reach.

**Relationship with leadership:** This is often the most politically complex engagement. The leadership team has been managing the operation for years — they believe they understand it. The operator's audit implies they don't. The key is to frame every finding as new information that became visible through the audit process, not as evidence that the leadership team was wrong.

**Day 1–30 requirement:** Map the system before diagnosing it. Stocks, flows, feedback loops. Where is information not reaching the people who need it? Where are standards being met on paper while the underlying objective degrades? Where are the workarounds that have become load-bearing?

**Failure mode:** Producing a comprehensive gap analysis without a constraint-prioritized action plan. Operational audits that list twenty problems without naming the one that limits everything else produce paralysis, not improvement.

### 4. The Pricing Reset

**What it is:** The business is undercharging. The operator's mandate is to redesign the pricing structure — and to do so without destroying the client relationships that exist at the old price.

**Constraint structure:** The constraint in a pricing engagement is almost always psychological, not mathematical. The math of the new pricing is easy. The psychology of communicating it — to existing clients, to a sales team that has built relationships on the old structure, to a founder who used low prices as a growth strategy — is the actual challenge.

**The Rider, Elephant, Path problem (Switch / Heath):** The Rider accepts the case for pricing reset immediately — the math is compelling. The Elephant (the sales team, the founder, the existing client) has built identity and relationships around the old price. Addressing the Rider without addressing the Elephant produces a pricing model that is approved in meetings and ignored in practice.

**Relationship with leadership:** The founder often has a complex relationship with pricing — particularly if they used low prices to build early relationships and now those relationships carry an implicit promise of continuation. The operator must understand the history before proposing the future.

**Day 1–30 requirement:** Audit the existing client base by value delivered vs. price paid. Identify the cohort most likely to support a price increase (highest engagement, clearest ROI) and the cohort most at risk (legacy pricing, relationship-dependent, low engagement). The audit produces a sequenced plan — not a blanket announcement.

**Dominant friction archetype:** Decision friction at the moment of client communication. The plan is clear; the conversation is hard. Crucial Conversations (Patterson) applies directly: establish mutual purpose before stating the price change. The client must understand that the change serves the relationship's sustainability, not just the operator's economics.

**Failure mode:** Implementing pricing as a system change without managing it as a relationship conversation. Clients who receive a price increase without a conversation become former clients.

### 5. The Team Rebuild

**What it is:** The leadership team is not performing. Some combination of wrong people, wrong roles, wrong relationships, and wrong dynamics is producing an organization that cannot execute the strategy.

**Constraint structure:** The constraint in a team rebuild is usually a single individual in a key role — the Herbie in the org chart. But identifying Herbie correctly requires separating performance from capability from fit. A person can be high-capability and wrong for the current stage. They can be low-performance and wrong-role. They can be right-capability and right-role and undermined by a relationship dynamic that's not visible on the org chart.

**Shared consciousness requirement (Team of Teams / McChrystal):** Before any personnel change, build the shared intelligence picture. Does the leadership team know what each other is working on? Do they share a mental model of the organization's operating environment? The team that lacks shared consciousness will not perform well even after personnel changes — because the root cause of the underperformance is structural, not individual.

**Relationship with leadership:** This is the highest-stakes relationship archetype. Personnel decisions affect people's livelihoods and professional identities. The operator who moves too fast on personnel changes before earning trust destroys the credibility that makes subsequent changes possible.

**Task-relevant maturity (Grove):** Apply TRM evaluation to each leadership team member for each critical function. A CFO who is high-TRM on financial reporting may be low-TRM on board communication. The evaluation is function-specific, not person-global. The management style must match the TRM level per function.

**Day 1–30 requirement:** One-on-ones with every direct report before any diagnosis is shared publicly. Listen. Form no public opinions. The opinions will be tested against what the data produces in Days 31–60.

**Failure mode:** Making personnel decisions before the constraint is correctly identified. Moving the wrong person removes a visible problem without addressing the structural cause — and creates a new problem when the replacement encounters the same system dynamics.

### 6. The PE-Backed Value Creation Engagement

**What it is:** The operator is working inside or alongside a PE-backed company, with explicit mandate to improve EBITDA ahead of an exit event.

**Financial frame (PE Playbook / Coffey · Financial Intelligence / Berman):** Every operational decision is ultimately an EBITDA decision. The three levers: revenue growth, margin expansion, multiple expansion. The operator who frames their work in EBITDA terms — and who understands the fund timeline that governs the PE firm's urgency — is operating in the language of the actual decision-makers.

**The 100-day plan logic applies directly.** The PE firm has a thesis about value creation that was built during diligence. The operator's first job is to understand that thesis — not to replace it with their own, but to evaluate whether it's correct and to identify which assumptions are at risk. Days 1–30: listen and assess. Days 31–60: diagnose and build the operating plan. Days 61–100: initiate the first wave.

**The clock is real.** The fund timeline is not a management preference — it's a financial structure. Five years into a ten-year fund means four years to exit. Every recommendation must fit within that window.

**Dominant friction archetype:** Execution friction created by the PE firm's quarterly reporting cadence conflicting with the operator's initiative timelines. Value creation initiatives that take 18 months to produce results are hard to sustain inside a board cycle that evaluates performance every 90 days. The operator must build a leading indicator cadence that demonstrates progress before results arrive.

**Failure mode:** Building a great operating plan that doesn't translate into EBITDA movement visible to the board within the fund timeline. The plan that improves the business but misses the exit window produces a good report card and a failed engagement.

## How PRAECEPTOR Uses This File

When the RESEARCHER brief identifies an engagement domain, PRAECEPTOR reads the brief through this archetype lens:

1. Which archetype (or combination) does this engagement most resemble?
2. What does the archetype's constraint structure imply about the 30/60/90 structure?
3. What does the archetype's relationship dynamic imply about the engagement's political landscape?
4. What is the archetype's dominant failure mode — and where in the plan does the operator most need to slow down rather than accelerate?

The 30/60/90 plan that follows is shaped by the archetype's answers, not by generic project management logic.
