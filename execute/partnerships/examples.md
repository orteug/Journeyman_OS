# Partnerships — Examples

## Example 1 — Building Shared Consciousness

**BAD**
The operator holds separate weekly check-ins with the VP of Operations, the VP of Sales, and the CFO. Each call is productive. The operator accumulates a rich picture of the organization. The three VPs do not interact during the engagement, and the operator is the only person who holds the full picture. When the operator's engagement ends, the shared picture disappears with them.

*What failed:* The operator became the intelligence hub rather than building the intelligence infrastructure. The organization is dependent on the operator's presence and exits the engagement without the shared consciousness that would have made the operator's work durable.

**GOOD**
At Week 2, the operator proposes a weekly 45-minute cross-functional sync: all three VPs in the same room (or call), each briefing the others on what they're seeing. The operator facilitates for the first two sessions and then steps back. By Week 6, the VPs are running the sync without the operator's agenda. The cross-functional intelligence picture persists after the engagement closes.

## Example 2 — High-Stakes Conversation

**BAD**
The operator's research reveals that the VP of Operations has been systematically underreporting a quality defect rate to protect her team. The operator prepares a slide in the board deck that presents the accurate defect rate alongside the previously reported figures. The VP sees the slide in the board meeting for the first time. The meeting becomes a defensive negotiation. The operator's credibility and the VP's trust are both damaged.

*What failed:* The operator surprised a principal. The defensiveness was predictable. The board meeting was the wrong venue for the VP to process the finding for the first time.

**GOOD**
Before finalizing the deck, the operator requests a private meeting with the VP: "I've found a discrepancy in the defect rate data that I need your help understanding before I present to the board. I want to make sure I'm reading it correctly." The VP has the opportunity to explain the context, offer additional data, and shape how the finding is presented. The board presentation reflects the corrected data, but the VP is in the room as a collaborator rather than a defendant.

## Example 3 — Empowered Execution

**BAD**
The operator establishes a daily check-in protocol for the client's operations lead to report progress on a 12-item action list. The operations lead sends a daily update email. The operator responds with adjustments. The operations lead waits for the response before acting on ambiguous items. The engagement runs at the speed of the operator's email response time.

*What failed:* The operator has communicated tasks but not intent. The operations lead has no framework for making decisions independently, so every ambiguity becomes a bottleneck.

**GOOD**
At the start of the phase, the operator spends 30 minutes with the operations lead establishing intent: "Here's what we're trying to achieve in the next 30 days and why it matters for the engagement. Within that, you have full authority to make any decision that (1) doesn't commit new spend above $5K, and (2) doesn't change the client relationship. Anything else, bring it to me — but only if you've already formed your own recommendation." The operations lead makes 11 of 12 decisions independently. The one that surfaces is genuinely escalation-worthy.
