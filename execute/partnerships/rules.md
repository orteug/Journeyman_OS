# Partnerships — Rules

## R1 — Build the shared picture before building the plan

An operator who enters an engagement and immediately starts directing will make decisions based on an incomplete picture of the operating environment. The client's functions don't share intelligence by default. The information that matters is distributed across people who don't talk to each other regularly.

Before the plan runs, create the mechanism for shared consciousness: the cross-functional briefing, the weekly intelligence sync, the deliberate cross-unit exposure. The operator who has the full picture makes fewer wrong calls. The operator who doesn't will correct the same mistake multiple times.

## R2 — Push decision authority to where the information is held

If every significant decision routes through the operator, the operator becomes the bottleneck. The engagement stalls at the operator's processing speed. This is the chess master failure: superior vision of the board combined with inability to scale because every move requires the master's attention.

The alternative: establish the intent clearly enough, and build trust deeply enough, that the client's team can act without asking. When someone asks "what should I do about X?" and X is within the established intent and their domain of responsibility, the answer is usually "what do you think?" — and then trust the call.

## R3 — Trust is built through embedding, not through meetings

Cross-functional trust — the kind that produces empowered execution — is not built in formal meetings. It is built through shared experience: working alongside, failing together, seeing how the other party handles pressure. Embedding one team member in another's function for a week produces more trust than six months of weekly syncs.

In every engagement, identify one cross-functional embedding opportunity. Propose it. The organizational resistance to it is itself useful information about the trust deficit.

## R4 — Silence is not agreement

When a crucial conversation surfaces and the other party goes quiet, they have not agreed — they have shut down. Silence is the safety response: disengagement to avoid the perceived threat of the conversation. Agreement produced under these conditions is not durable.

Restore safety before continuing. Name what you observe: "I notice we've moved away from the topic." Ask a genuine question about their view. Do not interpret silence as alignment and move on.

## R5 — Name the purpose before the position

In any high-stakes conversation where interests appear to conflict: name the mutual purpose before stating any position. "I'm raising this because I want the engagement to succeed and I think your perspective on this is critical" is different from "I think we need to change the approach." The first opens a conversation. The second opens a negotiation.

When mutual purpose is in doubt, create it explicitly: "What would a solution look like that works for both of us?"

## R6 — Low self-orientation is the operating system

Every relationship move in the engagement is contaminated if the operator's urgency is the filter. The client's situation comes first. The operator who needs a win before the relationship can support it destroys the relationship in the process.

The diagnostic: before any significant interaction with a client-side stakeholder, ask — whose agenda is driving this? If the answer is "mine," wait.
