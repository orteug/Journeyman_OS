# PE Operator Context
## Source: The Private Equity Playbook — Adam Coffey

When the client is PE-backed, the financial context changes. The operator who understands the fund structure, the value creation thesis, and the equity incentive structure is not working in the dark. They understand what the client's financial stakeholders actually care about — and can position the engagement's work accordingly.

---

## The Fund Structure and the Pressure It Creates

PE firms raise funds from institutional investors with a defined life — typically 10 years. The fund must invest the capital, grow the portfolio companies, and return capital to investors within that window.

The math that creates urgency: PE firms earn management fees (2% of committed capital per year) and carried interest (20% of profits above the hurdle rate). Their financial incentive is maximizing the multiple on invested capital at exit. Every year of hold time consumes fund life. PE firms are not patient capital.

**The operator's implication:** A PE-backed client's decision-making is governed by a clock the operator may not be able to see. Asking "where are you in the fund cycle?" is not intrusive — it is the question that makes the rest of the engagement context legible. A client five years into a ten-year fund and a client three years into a ten-year fund have different urgency profiles, different risk tolerances, and different receptivity to long-horizon recommendations.

---

## The Three Value Creation Levers

PE firms create value through three mechanisms, typically in sequence:

**1. Revenue Growth** — organic growth (new customers, pricing, geographic expansion) and inorganic growth (add-on acquisitions). Revenue growth at constant margins expands EBITDA directly.

**2. Margin Expansion** — reducing the cost structure relative to revenue. Every dollar of cost removed drops to EBITDA and compounds at the exit multiple. For the operator: margin expansion recommendations are the highest-leverage financial framing available.

**3. Multiple Expansion** — exiting at a higher EBITDA multiple than entry. Driven by: growing into a higher-multiple category, improving the quality of earnings (recurring revenue, diversified customer base, reduced key-person risk), and timing the exit to favorable market conditions.

The operator who frames their work against these three levers — "this reduces cost structure, expanding EBITDA margin by X basis points, which compounds at the expected exit multiple to Y dollars of exit value" — is speaking the language of the board and the sponsor.

## The 100-Day Plan Logic

Coffey's template for PE ownership transitions applies to operator engagements as well. The structure:

- **Days 1-30:** Listen and assess. Form no public opinions. One-on-ones with every direct report, review of financial statements, customer conversations.
- **Days 31-60:** Diagnose. Identify the three to five highest-leverage operational gaps. Build the initial operating plan.
- **Days 61-100:** Initiate. Begin the first wave of initiatives. Establish the operating rhythm. Signal the standards and pace that will govern the tenure.

The principle: move slowly in assessment, deliberately in diagnosis, decisively in initiation. The operator who announces changes before completing assessment damages the trust that makes implementation possible.

## What PE Firms Evaluate in Operators

Coffey's direct account of what PE operating partners look for:

- **Track record of execution:** Have they done the job before, at what scale, with what measurable results?
- **Financial fluency:** Can they read and interpret financial statements? Can they build a credible operating plan with financial projections?
- **Operator mentality:** Do they think like an owner — every dollar of cost is a dollar of EBITDA — or like an employee?
- **Board visibility:** Can they give board updates that are honest about underperformance and credible about the recovery plan?

The operator who demonstrates financial fluency during an engagement — who reads the P&L, challenges the assumptions in it, and frames recommendations in EBITDA terms — is being evaluated whether they know it or not.
