# Financial Intelligence During Engagement
## Source: Financial Intelligence — Karen Berman & Joe Knight

Financial statements are not objective truth. They are a set of estimates, assumptions, and choices made by accountants following rules — but always requiring judgment. The operator who treats the numbers as facts has a blind spot. The operator who understands the assumptions beneath the numbers has a structural advantage in every engagement where finances are relevant to the diagnosis.

---

## The Three Statements — What Each Actually Measures

**Income Statement (P&L)**
Measures *accounting profit* over a period — not cash generated. A company can show profit while burning cash. Key lines:
- **Gross profit:** Revenue minus cost of goods sold. The margin earned on core operations before overhead.
- **EBITDA:** Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, amortization. Strips out financing choices and accounting estimates — the closest proxy for what the business operationally generates. PE firms use it to value businesses.
- **Net income:** Bottom-line profit. Meaningful for comparison, but most affected by non-operational factors (financing, tax jurisdiction, depreciation schedule).

**Balance Sheet**
A snapshot of what the company owns (assets) and owes (liabilities) at a single point in time. The difference is equity.
- **Working capital:** Current assets minus current liabilities. The operational liquidity cushion.
- **Days Sales Outstanding (DSO):** Accounts receivable ÷ (Revenue ÷ 365). How long it takes to collect on a sale. Rising DSO is one of the earliest signals of customer distress or collections failure.

**Cash Flow Statement**
Measures actual cash movement — in and out. The statement most operators skip and the one that predicts survival. Profitable companies go bankrupt when receivables grow faster than collections or when working capital expansion outpaces operating cash flow. The signal is here, not in the P&L.

## The Art in the Numbers — Where Judgment Lives

**Revenue recognition timing.** When is revenue recorded — at contract signing, delivery, or cash receipt? Different choices produce different profit levels from identical economics. A company recognizing revenue at signing looks more profitable in growth periods than one recognizing at delivery.

**Depreciation estimates.** A company that depreciates aggressively (shorter useful life) shows lower short-term profits and higher long-term ones. This is why EBITDA strips D&A — to remove the estimation choice from the performance comparison.

**One-time items.** Restructuring charges and asset write-offs are supposed to be non-recurring. Companies that take frequent "one-time" charges are managing reported earnings. The operator reads through them to find underlying operating performance.

## EBITDA — The Engagement Scorecard Metric

For PE-backed clients and mid-market operators thinking about exit value: every operational decision should be evaluated against its EBITDA impact.

The multiplication effect: a business generating $2M EBITDA at a 6x exit multiple is worth $12M. Adding $300K of EBITDA through an operational improvement adds $1.8M of exit value — at no additional cost of capital. This framing converts operational recommendations into financial outcomes that the client's financial stakeholders can evaluate and act on.

## The Senior Operator's Read

- "Revenue is growing" → junior operator
- "Revenue is growing, but gross margin is declining — there's a cost structure issue" → senior operator
- "We're profitable" → junior operator
- "We're profitable but DSO has risen 40 days — the cash position will tighten in 60 days" → senior operator
- "EBITDA is $2M" → junior operator
- "Reported EBITDA is $2M but $400K of that is recurring 'one-time' items — normalized EBITDA is $1.6M" → senior operator
