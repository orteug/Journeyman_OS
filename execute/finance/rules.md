# Finance — Rules

## R1 — Read the cash flow statement first

Profit is an opinion. Cash is a fact. The income statement tells you whether the client's accountants have classified revenue and expenses in a way that produces a positive number. The cash flow statement tells you whether the business is actually generating or consuming cash.

A client that is profitable and burning cash has a constraint the P&L obscures. The operator who reads the income statement first will miss it. Start with cash flow.

## R2 — Find the assumptions before trusting the numbers

Every financial statement contains management's choices embedded as facts. Revenue recognition timing, depreciation schedule, useful life estimates, one-time item classification — these are decisions, not measurements. Two companies with identical operations can report different profit levels based on their accountants' choices.

Before drawing any operational conclusion from a financial statement, ask: what would have to be true for this number to look different? The assumption that changes the number the most is the assumption worth understanding.

## R3 — Translate every operational decision into EBITDA impact

In most mid-market and PE-backed engagements, EBITDA is the scorecard metric. Every dollar of cost removed drops to EBITDA and compounds at the exit multiple. Every dollar of revenue added at constant margin does the same.

The operator who frames their work in EBITDA terms — not just operational terms — speaks the language of the client's financial stakeholders. "This will save $300K in annual labor cost" is an operational statement. "This expands EBITDA margin by 200 basis points, which compounds at a 6x exit multiple to $1.8M in exit value" is a financial statement. They describe the same thing. One lands with a PE-backed board.

## R4 — Track DSO as a leading indicator of client distress

Days Sales Outstanding — how long it takes the client to collect on a sale — is one of the most reliable early signals of downstream financial stress. Rising DSO means either the client's customers are having trouble paying (credit/demand signal) or the client's collections process is weak (operational signal). Either is worth surfacing.

A DSO that has risen quietly from 45 to 90 days while revenue grew will eventually produce a cash crisis that the income statement did not warn about.

## R5 — Know the fund timeline before the first deliverable

If the client is PE-backed: find out how far into the fund's life they are. A company acquired three years into a ten-year fund has seven years. A company acquired six years in has four. The fund timeline governs the urgency of the PE firm's expectations and, by extension, the urgency of the engagement. An operator who doesn't know the timeline is working without a critical context variable.

## R6 — Financial intelligence is interpretation, not advice

The operator uses financial intelligence to understand the client's situation and to make better recommendations. They do not produce financial models, advise on tax strategy, or make representations about the accuracy of the client's financial statements. When the engagement surfaces a financial issue that requires professional financial advice, name it and route to the appropriate professional.
