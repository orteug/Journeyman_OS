# Finance — Examples

## Example 1 — Reading the Numbers

**BAD**
The client's CEO shows the operator the Q3 P&L. Revenue is up 22%. EBITDA is up 18%. The CEO is pleased. The operator nods and moves on to operational priorities. Three weeks later, the client's CFO reports a cash flow problem that requires an emergency credit line draw.

*What failed:* The operator read the income statement but not the cash flow statement. Revenue growth was outpacing collections — DSO had risen from 48 to 81 days over 9 months, quietly consuming cash while the P&L looked healthy. The signal was in the balance sheet and cash flow statement, not the income statement the CEO highlighted.

**GOOD**
Before the Q3 review meeting, the operator reads all three statements. Notes DSO trend from the balance sheet — receivables have grown disproportionate to revenue. In the meeting: "Revenue looks strong. One thing I'm watching — receivables are growing faster than revenue, which means the cash conversion cycle is lengthening. What's driving the DSO increase?" The CFO acknowledges a collections backlog on three large accounts. The operator routes this as a financial constraint to address before it becomes a crisis.

## Example 2 — EBITDA Translation

**BAD**
The operator identifies a process inefficiency that could reduce headcount by 2 FTEs. They present it as: "This would save approximately $160,000 in annual labor costs." The PE firm's operating partner nods but doesn't engage. The recommendation moves slowly through approval.

*What failed:* The operator communicated in operational terms to a financially-oriented audience. The PE firm thinks in multiples, not in savings.

**GOOD**
"This would save approximately $160,000 in annual labor costs — which is roughly 100 basis points of EBITDA margin improvement. At the current 6x forward multiple, that's approximately $960,000 of additional exit value. It's a one-time implementation cost of $40,000 and a 12-week timeline." The PE operating partner asks when they can start.

## Example 3 — PE Context

**BAD**
The operator delivers a 12-month transformation roadmap to a PE-backed client that is 5 years into a 10-year fund cycle. The roadmap is technically sound. The operating partner pushes back hard. The operator is confused — the work is good.

*What failed:* The operator didn't read the fund context. Five years into the fund means the PE firm needs to demonstrate returns and position for exit. A 12-month roadmap with results in month 10 is, from the PE firm's perspective, a plan that delays the exit. The operating partner's urgency wasn't about the work — it was about the timeline.

**GOOD**
Before building the roadmap, the operator asks: "Where are you in the fund cycle, and what does the exit timeline look like?" Learns the firm is targeting a 24-month exit window. Rebuilds the roadmap as a 90-day acceleration layer followed by a 12-month value-creation plan — leading with the EBITDA levers that are fastest to realize. The operating partner becomes a sponsor of the engagement.
