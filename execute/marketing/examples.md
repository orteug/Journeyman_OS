# Marketing — Examples

## Example 1 — Addressing the Elephant

**BAD**
The operator's research finds that the client's pricing model has been undercharging enterprise customers by approximately 30% for three years. The recommendation deck opens with: "Current pricing is below market by 30%, representing approximately $2.4M in annual unrealized revenue." The CEO receives the finding and responds that the pricing has always been competitive and the model has been carefully developed. The deck goes into a file.

*What failed:* The finding implicitly criticizes three years of the CEO's pricing decisions. The Rider accepted the logic. The Elephant heard a personal challenge and shut down. The recommendation never had a chance.

**GOOD**
The operator shares the headline privately with the CEO before the deck: "The market benchmarking surfaced something I want your perspective on before I present to the team. It looks like enterprise pricing has stayed flat while market comparables have moved up. I want to understand the rationale first — there may be strategic context I'm missing, or it may be an opportunity." The CEO explains there was a deliberate growth-stage discounting strategy that was never formally closed out. The operator reframes the recommendation as "completing the transition from growth pricing to market pricing" — which is what the CEO intended but never operationalized. The deck lands as a shared plan rather than a critique.

## Example 2 — Shaping the Path

**BAD**
The operator recommends that the client's sales team shift from relationship-based pipeline management to a structured CRM-first process. The presentation is thorough: data on pipeline predictability, benchmark comparisons, three-year revenue impact. The sales leadership agrees in the meeting. Ninety days later, the CRM adoption rate is 23%. The recommendation failed.

*What failed:* The argument changed. The environment didn't. The CRM was implemented but the surrounding conditions — the commission structure, the reporting format, the sales leadership's own behavior — still rewarded the old approach. The Elephant went with the path it knew.

**GOOD**
The operator identifies three environmental changes that would make CRM use easier than not: (1) eliminate the weekly verbal pipeline report and replace it with a CRM export, (2) tie deal stage credit to CRM entry rather than verbal update, (3) have sales leadership enter their own deals in the CRM visibly for 30 days. None of these require anyone to be persuaded. They require the environment to change. Adoption reaches 81% in 90 days.

## Example 3 — The Early Win

**BAD**
The operator spends the first 45 days of a 90-day engagement in research, analysis, and plan development. The client's senior team has seen no output. At Day 45, the operator delivers a comprehensive diagnostic document. The response is polite but tepid. The team has spent 45 days forming impressions of the engagement without any evidence of value.

*What failed:* The operator let the positioning void fill itself. In the absence of visible output, the client team formed their own assessments — some skeptical, some simply uncertain. The comprehensive diagnostic arrived into a credibility deficit.

**GOOD**
At Day 14, the operator surfaces one specific, actionable finding from the early research: a pricing discrepancy on a single product line that can be corrected immediately with measurable revenue impact. They present it informally to the CEO: "I'm still in early research, but I found something worth acting on now — do you want me to walk you through it?" The CEO approves a quick fix. By Day 30, the correction has generated $40K in additional revenue. The comprehensive diagnostic delivered at Day 45 lands into a room that is already convinced the engagement is worth something.
