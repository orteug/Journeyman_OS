# Marketing — Rules

## R1 — Address the Elephant, not just the Rider

The rational case for a recommendation (the Rider) is necessary but not sufficient. The emotional brain (the Elephant) determines whether the behavior actually changes. A recommendation that is analytically compelling but emotionally threatening will be accepted in the meeting and ignored in practice.

Before delivering any significant finding: identify the emotional dimension. What does this finding imply about the client's past decisions? About their team? About their identity as a leader? A finding that implicitly criticizes past choices must be framed as new information that wasn't available, not as evidence of a mistake. The Rider accepts the logic either way. The Elephant only moves if it isn't threatened.

## R2 — Shape the path, not just the argument

The biggest source of change failures is operators who change the argument without changing the environment. If the old behavior is the default — built into processes, incentives, tools, and expectations — the new behavior will revert regardless of how compelling the case for it is.

After every recommendation: identify what would have to change in the environment to make the new behavior easier than the old one. This is the Path. A recommendation without a path is a persuasion exercise. A recommendation with a path is an operational change.

## R3 — Find the bright spots before prescribing the fix

In any client organization, some parts are already working the way the engagement wants the whole to work. Find them. Name them. Understand what makes them work, and replicate the conditions rather than importing a solution from outside.

The bright spots approach is faster than wholesale change, politically less threatening (it validates what's working), and more likely to stick (it's adapted from what the organization already produces, not imposed from outside). It is also strong intelligence: the conditions that produce the bright spots are the levers.

## R4 — Shrink the change to the smallest credible first step

The larger the change, the more the Elephant resists. An organization asked to change everything changes nothing. An organization asked to change one specific, visible thing in the next two weeks has a tractable challenge.

Every recommendation has a smallest credible first step. Identify it. Make it explicit. Make it time-bounded. The first step is not the whole change — it is the demonstration that the change is possible, which creates the momentum for the steps that follow.

## R5 — Early wins are not optional

The operator's positioning inside the client organization is built incrementally through demonstrated competence. An engagement that moves from research to plan to deliverable without any visible win in the first 30 days is losing credibility in the silence.

Identify the earliest point in the engagement plan where a tangible, visible result is possible. Prioritize it. The early win is not a distraction from the long-term work — it is the credibility investment that makes the long-term work land.

## R6 — Never surprise a principal

Senior client-side stakeholders (CEO, board member, PE principal) should never encounter a finding from the operator for the first time in a formal setting. The surprise produces a defensive response that damages both the finding and the relationship.

Before any significant deliverable to a principal: share the headline with them privately first. Ask for their reaction. Adjust the framing where needed. The finding doesn't change — the delivery does. A principal who has already processed the headline and shaped their response will be an ally in the formal setting rather than a skeptic.
