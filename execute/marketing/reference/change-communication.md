# Change Communication
## Source: Switch — Chip Heath & Dan Heath

Heath & Heath's central insight: change is hard not because people are resistant by nature but because the rational brain and the emotional brain have different needs — and most change communication addresses only one of them. The framework: three forces shape every behavioral change, and every one must be addressed.

---

## The Rider, the Elephant, and the Path

**The Rider** — the rational mind. Analytical, deliberate, planning-oriented. The Rider can evaluate a recommendation logically and accept it as correct. The Rider is also prone to analysis paralysis: when the path forward is ambiguous, the Rider will study rather than act.

**The Elephant** — the emotional brain. Motivated by feeling, not logic. The Elephant is the engine: it provides the energy and will for any sustained behavior change. When the Elephant doesn't want to move, the Rider can analyze all day and the behavior won't change. When the Elephant is motivated, it can power through significant friction.

**The Path** — the environment. The structural conditions that make a behavior easy or hard. If the old behavior is built into the workflow, the tools, the incentives, and the expectations, the new behavior will revert no matter how compelling the case for it is. The path determines what happens when the Rider and Elephant are both pointing in the right direction but the environment points back to the old way.

---

## Applying the Framework in Engagements

**For the Rider:** Provide direction, not motivation.
- Give a clear destination: not "improve the process" but "the defect rate reaches 1.5% by Day 60."
- Provide a rationale that the Rider can follow: why this milestone, why this metric, why this timeline.
- Don't over-analyze. Analysis paralysis sets in when the destination is ambiguous. Clarity on the outcome frees the Rider to start moving.

**For the Elephant:** Provide motivation, not just logic.
- Find the emotional connection: what does this change mean for the people the client's team cares about? For their customers? For their team's sense of competence?
- Shrink the change: a large change overwhelms the Elephant. Break it into the smallest credible first step. "Just run the process once, with two customers, this week." The Elephant can handle a step that feels manageable.
- Find the bright spots: what is already working the way the change wants everything to work? Identify it. Amplify it. The Elephant gains confidence from evidence that the destination is reachable.

**For the Path:** Change the environment, not just the argument.
- Remove friction from the new behavior. If the new behavior requires more effort than the old one, most people will choose the old one — not because they're lazy but because effort is a cost.
- Build in defaults: the new behavior becomes what happens automatically unless someone actively chooses otherwise.
- Rally the herd: people watch what other people do. When the visible, respected members of the client's team demonstrate the new behavior, the environmental signal shifts. Behavior is contagious.

---

## The Bright Spots Method

Before prescribing a change, look for where the desired end state already exists — even partially. In any client organization, some teams, some individuals, or some processes are already performing the way the engagement wants the whole to perform.

These bright spots are: faster to scale than external solutions (because they're already adapted to the organization's context), politically safer to raise (because they validate rather than criticize), and more durable (because they're built on what the organization already produces).

The bright spots inquiry: "Where is this already working? What are the conditions that produce it? How do we replicate those conditions?" This is not a backup strategy when the direct approach fails. It is often the fastest path to organizational change.

---

## Shrinking the Change

The most common reason organizational change fails is that the first step is too large. The Elephant, facing an overwhelming task, does nothing rather than starting something it isn't sure it can finish.

Every recommendation has a smallest credible first step — the version of the change that is small enough to be done this week, visible enough to count as evidence, and significant enough to build toward the real goal. Find it. Make it explicit. Make it the assignment, not the end state.

The first step matters more than it appears to: it produces evidence that the change is possible, creates momentum in the Elephant, and gives the Rider a clear action rather than a vague aspiration.
