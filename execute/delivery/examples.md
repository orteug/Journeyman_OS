# Delivery — Examples

## Example 1 — Setting Urgency

**BAD**
The operator arrives at the Day 30 check-in and asks how things are going. The client reports that the discovery interviews took longer than expected but are mostly done. The operator nods and moves the milestone timeline by two weeks. The engagement is now running at the client's pace.

*What failed:* The operator absorbed the client's energy level rather than naming the gap. A two-week slip in discovery compresses every downstream milestone. The accommodation feels respectful but guarantees a worse result.

**GOOD**
At the Day 30 check-in, the operator reads the milestone status against the plan. "We're at Day 30 and discovery is 60% complete. The plan needs 100% by Day 30 to keep the architecture work on schedule. What specifically created the delay, and what changes in the next 10 days to close it?" The client provides the answer. The operator holds the timeline and agrees to a specific recovery plan with a check-in at Day 40.

## Example 2 — Communicating Direction

**BAD**
The operator sends a detailed task list to the client's operations lead: "Please complete the following: (1) Pull Q1-Q3 revenue by product line from the ERP. (2) Export the headcount by department as of 9/30. (3) Get me the last three board decks." The operations lead asks clarifying questions for two days and produces half the list.

*What failed:* The operator communicated tasks, not intent. The operations lead had no understanding of why any of it was needed, so every ambiguity required the operator's attention. The operator became the bottleneck.

**GOOD**
"I'm building a picture of where revenue is growing and where headcount grew to support it — looking for gaps where costs scaled faster than revenue. If you can pull anything from the ERP that shows revenue trend and headcount trend by business unit over the last three quarters, that gives me what I need. If the ERP doesn't slice it that way, what's the next best source?" The operations lead comes back with a solution the operator hadn't considered — and doesn't need a follow-up.

## Example 3 — Managing WIP

**BAD**
At the Day 45 cadence review, the operator counts 14 items in progress across the engagement: a pricing analysis, a process map, three customer interviews, two vendor reviews, a draft recommendation deck, an org chart update, and four action items from the previous meeting. Nothing is complete. The client reports that "a lot is happening."

*What failed:* The engagement has WIP spread across too many fronts. Nothing is finishing. The milestone deliverables are buried under activity. The operator allowed scope to accumulate rather than enforcing a completion discipline.

**GOOD**
At Day 45, the operator opens the cadence review by reading the WIP board: 5 items in progress, 4 complete since last week. "Before we add anything new today, I want to finish two of the current five. Which two are closest to done, and what does each need in the next 48 hours?" Two items get owners and deadlines. Nothing new enters until something exits.
