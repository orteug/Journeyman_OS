# Mission Command
## Source: The Art of Action — Stephen Bungay

Bungay's central insight: the gap between what leaders plan and what organizations deliver is not a failure of execution discipline — it is a structural consequence of how information and authority are separated in traditional hierarchies. Plans fail because the plan-writer lacks the on-the-ground information to make the plan accurate, and the on-the-ground actor lacks the authority to adapt when reality diverges from the plan. The solution is not better planning. It is a different doctrine: mission command.

---

## The Three Gaps

Every plan-to-execution translation contains three inevitable gaps:

**Knowledge gap:** The plan-writer does not know what the people executing it know. Ground conditions, emerging constraints, and local opportunities are invisible from the planning level. A plan that specifies how to act cannot account for what the executor sees that the planner cannot.

**Alignment gap:** The organization does not always do what the plan says, because the intent behind the plan is not clear enough to make judgment calls in novel situations. When something unexpected happens and the plan is silent, people default to asking upward or doing nothing.

**Effects gap:** The plan produces effects other than the intended one, because real systems are adaptive. The market, the competitor, the client, the team — they respond to the plan, and the plan did not account for their responses.

The operator who tries to close these gaps through more detailed planning makes the problem worse. More instructions create more alignment points to maintain and more places for the organization to wait for guidance.

---

## The Mission Command Alternative

**Communicate intent, not instructions.** Every directive from the operator states: what the desired outcome is (the "what"), and why it matters (the "why"). The how is left to the person closest to the situation. The outcome-plus-rationale gives the executor enough understanding to make judgment calls the operator did not anticipate.

**Directed opportunism.** When the situation diverges from the plan — and it will — the executor does not wait for new instructions. They ask: given the intent I understand, what is the right move here? Directed opportunism is not improvisation. It is the application of understood intent to novel circumstances.

**Backbrief.** After communicating intent, the operator asks the executor to state back what they understood — not as a comprehension check, but because the act of stating intent often surfaces misalignment that the original communication didn't reveal. The backbrief is not adversarial. It is the mechanism that closes the alignment gap before it becomes an execution failure.

---

## Applied to Engagements

The operator running a 90-day engagement is the plan-writer. The client's team — the people who will execute most of the work — are the ground-level actors. The mission command question at the start of every phase: does the team understand what we are trying to achieve well enough to make the right call when I'm not in the room?

If the answer is no, the operator has communicated tasks. Tasks cannot be adapted. Intent can.
