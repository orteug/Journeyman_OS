# Execution Urgency
## Source: Amp It Up — Frank Slootman

Amplitude is the intensity of the signal you bring to the organization. Not volume — not talking loudly or projecting confidence. Intensity of expectation, urgency, and engagement.

Slootman's observation: most organizations run at 40-60% of their potential not because they lack strategy or resources but because their leaders have calibrated to the organization's current performance level and stopped demanding more. The tolerance of substandard becomes the standard. Amplitude is the antidote.

---

## The Three Levers

**Raise standards.** Standards are what the team is expected to deliver as a minimum, not as an exceptional result. Most organizations have aspirational standards (what they say they want) and operational standards (what they actually accept). The gap between the two is the amplitude deficit. Closing it requires the operator to name, consistently, when operational standards fall short of aspirational ones — and to not move on until the gap is addressed.

**Increase urgency.** Urgency is the operating tempo. Most organizations run at a pace calibrated to their slowest constraint, their most cautious stakeholder, or their most politicized decision. Increasing urgency means compressing timelines by design — asking "why does this take three weeks?" rather than accepting three weeks as the natural cycle time. The answer is usually a combination of habit, queue depth, and the absence of anyone who cares enough to accelerate.

**Narrow focus.** Amplitude dissipates across too many priorities. An organization pursuing twelve initiatives at 40% effort produces worse results than one pursuing three initiatives at full intensity. The operator's job is to narrow the engagement's focus to what actually moves the milestones and to resist the organizational pressure to work on everything simultaneously.

---

## Urgency Calibration During Engagement

The client's ambient urgency is a lagging indicator — it reflects how fast the organization has historically moved, not how fast the engagement requires it to move. The operator who matches the client's ambient pace has surrendered agenda control.

Calibration test: at any given point in the engagement, ask — if this milestone were not delivered on time, what would not happen? If the answer is "not much" or "we'd adjust the timeline," urgency has not been established. If the answer names a specific consequence that the client cares about, urgency is real.

The operator establishes real urgency by connecting milestones to consequences the client owns — not to the operator's timeline preference.

---

## What Amplitude Is Not

Amplitude is not aggression or intimidation. It is not the operator's energy level substituting for the team's engagement. It is not maintaining high standards on the dimensions the operator cares about while tolerating drift on the dimensions the client controls.

High amplitude in an engagement looks like: consistently naming when progress does not match the plan, holding the milestone timeline even when accommodation is the path of least resistance, and treating the client's team as capable of delivering the plan rather than managing them to a lower bar out of courtesy.
