# Flow Management
## Source: Making Work Visible — Dominica DeGrandis

DeGrandis's core argument: knowledge work underdelivers not because people aren't working hard enough but because the work itself is invisible. Invisible work means invisible constraints. Invisible constraints cannot be managed. The discipline of making work visible is the precondition for managing it.

---

## The Five Thieves of Time

Five patterns that steal time from knowledge workers — invisibly, because the work itself is invisible.

**Thief 1 — Too Much WIP (Work In Progress)**
WIP is every piece of work that has been started but not finished. In knowledge work, WIP accumulates invisibly — there's no physical pile on the floor to signal the problem. But the cost is real: each additional item in progress increases context-switching, coordination overhead, and the probability that nothing finishes. The cure is not faster execution — it is less WIP.

**Thief 2 — Unknown Dependencies**
Work waits on other work that isn't on anyone's radar. A deliverable that appears to be 80% complete is actually blocked because it depends on a decision from a stakeholder who hasn't been brought into the process. Hidden dependencies are the most common reason that "we're almost done" conversations are false.

**Thief 3 — Unplanned Work**
Work that arrives without warning and displaces planned work. Every unplanned item is a prioritization event the team didn't have time to make properly. In engagements: the ad-hoc request from a senior stakeholder, the emergency analysis, the "quick question" that takes a day. Each one is legitimate on its own; accumulated, they destroy the planned work's timeline.

**Thief 4 — Conflicted Priorities**
When everything is high priority, nothing is. Team members with conflicting directives from different sources context-switch between them, make no progress on any of them, and eventually wait for someone else to decide. The operator's job is to hold a clear priority order so the team does not have to arbitrate it.

**Thief 5 — Neglected Work**
Partially complete work that has been set aside and not formally abandoned. Neglected work consumes the psychic space of "things that need to be done" without producing any output. The cure is a deliberate decision: finish, formally abandon, or descope. Not deferral.

---

## WIP Limits in Practice

WIP limits are not aspirational — they are the discipline that makes everything else work. The rule: no new work enters a category until something exits it.

In an engagement context: the operator agrees with the client team at the start of each phase on the maximum number of active items per person or per function. When a new item arrives, the conversation is: what comes out? If nothing can come out, the new item enters a queue — not the active board.

---

## Queue Time as Hidden Cost

Every item waiting in a queue is accumulating hidden cost: the delay in the delivery it enables, the decisions that cannot be made without it, and the psychological overhead of tracking it as "pending." Queue time is usually invisible because the item is not being worked on and therefore not consuming anyone's active attention — but it is consuming calendar time.

The operator who makes queue time visible — by tracking it and reporting it in cadence reviews — creates a different conversation about capacity and priority than the operator who only tracks active work.
