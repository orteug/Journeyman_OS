# Execution Cadence
## Source: High Output Management — Andy Grove

Grove's central definition: a manager's output is the output of their organizational unit plus the output of neighboring organizations they influence. Not their own work. Not their activities. The output of the system they manage.

This reframe is the foundation of execution cadence: the operator's job is not to do the work — it is to run the system that produces the work. Cadence is how that system maintains its operating rhythm.

---

## Leverage — The Decision Rule for the Operator's Time

Every managerial activity has a leverage ratio: the change in output produced per unit of time invested.

**High-leverage activities:**
- Setting direction clearly enough that the team makes the right calls without asking
- The one-on-one that identifies a problem before it becomes a crisis
- A decision that unblocks multiple downstream activities
- Building a process that makes a repeating task unnecessary to manage

**Low-leverage activities:**
- Doing work a team member could do (becoming the bottleneck)
- Attending meetings where presence doesn't change any outcome
- Making decisions that a team member has the information to make

**Negative-leverage activities:**
- Indecision that blocks multiple downstream activities
- Direction so vague that team members re-confirm before acting
- Reviewing work so closely that team members stop exercising judgment

The operating rule during engagement: each week, identify the highest-leverage activities available. Allocate time to those. Delegate or drop everything else.

## Task-Relevant Maturity — Who Gets What Kind of Management

The right management style for a given person depends on their maturity for the specific task, not their general seniority.

Task-relevant maturity (TRM) combines: relevant skill level, experience with this specific type of work, and demonstrated reliability in this context.

| TRM Level | Appropriate Style |
|-----------|------------------|
| Low | Structured, directive — tell them what to do and how |
| Medium | Communication-intensive — explain why, invite input |
| High | Minimal management — define the output, get out of the way |

The failure modes: managing a high-TRM person directively (micromanagement) kills performance. Managing a low-TRM person delegatively (abandonment) produces errors. The operator's job is to assess TRM correctly per task type — not per person globally.

## The Weekly Review — The Engagement's Heartbeat

The highest-leverage recurring meeting in any engagement. Not a status update — a decision-making venue. Done well:

- Each active workstream reports: what moved, what is blocked, what requires a decision
- The operator surfaces the one or two decisions that the week requires
- Action items exit the meeting with specific owners and deadlines
- Any item that produced "a lot happened but nothing is done" is named as a WIP problem, not a progress problem

The failure mode: the review becomes a progress report. The operator learns what happened but no decision is made and no action is owned. That is a social event at organizational cost.

---

## Indicators — Managing Before the Failure Is Visible

Manage with leading indicators, not just lagging ones. Leading indicators give time to correct; lagging ones confirm what already happened.

Pair every metric with a counter-metric to prevent gaming. Measure speed and quality. Measure volume and accuracy. Keep the indicator set small — too many metrics diffuse attention and produce optimization of the metric set rather than the underlying system.

The engagement's leading indicators are what tell the operator whether the milestones will be met before the milestone date arrives. Build them in Phase 1 of the engagement. Check them weekly. They are the instruments.
