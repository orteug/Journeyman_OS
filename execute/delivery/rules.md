# Delivery — Rules

## R1 — Urgency is not the client's to set

The operator sets the pace of the engagement. If the operator matches the client's ambient urgency, the engagement drifts to the client's tolerance for slowness. The plan has a timeline. The timeline has a logic. Running at lower amplitude than the plan requires is a choice to deliver a worse result.

When the client's urgency drops, name it: "We're at Day 45. The milestone for this phase requires X. We have 15 days. What's blocking?" That is not pressure — it is accountability. The engagement cannot hold if the operator absorbs the client's energy level rather than supplying their own.

## R2 — Communicate outcome and intent, not instructions

Every direction the operator gives must answer: what does success look like, and why does it matter? The client's team does not need a task list. They need enough clarity on the destination that they can make the right call in situations the operator has not anticipated.

If a team member asks "what should I do?" more than twice for the same class of problem, the intent has not been communicated clearly. Fix the intent statement, not the team member's judgment.

## R3 — Name all the work before managing any of it

Invisible work is unmanaged work. Before making any decision about priorities or capacity, the operator must know what is in progress — all of it. Undeclared WIP, hidden commitments, and work-in-queue that hasn't been counted yet will surface as crises.

The discipline: at the start of each engagement phase, map all the work. Not what should be happening — what is actually in flight. The surprise is always something that was already running.

## R4 — WIP limits are not optional

Finishing beats starting. An engagement with 12 things in progress and 3 completed is slower than one with 5 things in progress and 10 completed. WIP creates switching cost, coordination overhead, and the illusion of progress. The operator enforces WIP limits even when the client wants to add scope.

When scope addition is proposed: either something comes out, or the new item goes to the backlog. The engagement's capacity is fixed. The plan's integrity depends on this.

## R5 — Measure output, not activity

The engagement has milestones, not task lists. The deliverable that matters is the milestone output — not the number of meetings held, documents written, or hours logged. Activity without output is waste.

The cadence review asks: what has moved toward a milestone since the last review? If the answer is "a lot happened but nothing is done," the engagement has an activity problem disguised as a progress problem.

## R6 — Every cadence meeting ends with a decision

The recurring rhythm — weekly review, biweekly check-in, whatever cadence the engagement requires — is not a status update. It is a decision-making venue. If the meeting ends without a decision, it has not produced its output.

Before each cadence meeting: identify the decision that needs to be made. The rest of the meeting is the information-gathering that makes that decision possible.
