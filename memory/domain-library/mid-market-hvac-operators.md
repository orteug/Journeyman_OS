# Domain Library — Mid-Market HVAC Service Operators
*Created: 2025-09-14 | Last updated: 2025-12-03 | Engagements: 1 complete, 1 active*

---

## Domain Overview

**What this domain is:** Independent and regional HVAC service companies serving commercial and light industrial clients. Typically $3M–$30M revenue, 15–80 technicians, family-owned or owner-operated, serving 2-4 metro areas.

**Why this domain is distinct from residential HVAC:** Commercial HVAC is B2B with long-term maintenance contracts as the primary recurring revenue vehicle. Residential HVAC is transactional and highly seasonal. The business models, constraint structures, and pricing dynamics are different enough to treat them as separate domains.

---

## Structural Dynamics (Systems Lens)

**The core reinforcing loop:** Certified technicians → quality service delivery → contract retention → revenue stability → ability to offer competitive technician compensation → technician retention. This loop runs in both directions. Companies where it's running backward (turnover → service quality decline → contract losses → revenue pressure → can't compete on compensation → more turnover) are in a collapse spiral that looks like a sales problem but is actually a labor constraint problem.

**The bottleneck:** Certified technicians. NATE certification takes 2+ years of field experience and exam passage. There is no fast path. Technician supply is constrained by training time, not by compensation alone. The company that solves for technician retention rather than technician recruitment wins.

**The seasonal cash flow trap:** Revenue is reasonably consistent (maintenance contracts are annual), but demand peaks in summer (cooling) and winter (heating). The shoulder seasons (spring and fall) are revenue troughs — maintenance renewals slow, installation projects pause, emergency calls decline. But payroll continues. Companies that don't model this on a 13-week rolling cash basis get surprised every April and October.

**The pricing drift trap:** Maintenance contract pricing in this domain has been stable or declining in real terms for 5–7 years. Most operators have not raised prices since 2019–2020. The underlying cost structure (technician wages, equipment, fuel) has increased 25–35% over the same period. The gap between what the market will bear and what operators are charging is significant — but operators fear client departure and don't surface the conversation. This is the Crucial Conversations problem embedded in the domain structure.

## Operator Ground Truth
*What has been learned about this domain from direct engagement — not from external sources.*

**Technician retention is the constraint, not recruitment.** Every operator in this domain talks about "finding technicians." The actual problem is keeping them. The companies that have solved recruitment but not retention are running faster on the treadmill. The companies that have solved retention don't need to recruit. Retention drivers (from r/HVAC T1 signal + operator interview, 2025-09): 1) Trust that the shop will back them when there's a customer dispute, 2) Equipment investment — technicians who work with quality tools feel the company values the work, 3) Predictable scheduling (technicians with families value predictability over overtime premium), 4) Clear path to senior tech / crew lead (career ceiling is a retention risk).

**Pricing conversation structure that works.** From Engagement 1 outcome: the framing that landed was "service tier alignment" — presenting the price increase as bringing the client's service package current with their current needs and the current cost of delivering it, not as an increase in what the operator needs. The mutual purpose established first: "I want to make sure you have a maintenance program that actually protects your equipment through the next contract period. Let me show you what that looks like." Price discussed third, after value was established.

**Seasonal cash flow: the 13-week model.** Operators who model annual cash flow look fine on paper all year and get surprised in April. The 13-week rolling model shows the shoulder season cash trough 6–8 weeks before it arrives — enough time to draw the credit line strategically rather than reactively. One implementation note from Engagement 1: the credit facility needs to be established during the summer peak, when cash looks strong, not in October when the trough is arriving. Banks respond to strength, not urgency.

## What Research Has Not Resolved

- **True market pricing ceiling for maintenance contracts in this region.** The operator believes there is headroom above current pricing. Competitors have not publicly moved. The T1 signal from forums is suggestive but not definitive. Needs direct customer willingness-to-pay research (structured conversations with 5–8 current clients about value perception).
- **Digital channel effectiveness for commercial HVAC lead generation.** There is no T1 signal on whether Google Ads, content marketing, or referral programs produce better quality commercial leads in this domain. This is a gap in the domain library.
